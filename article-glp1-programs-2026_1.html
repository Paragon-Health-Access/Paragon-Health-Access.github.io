What Happened — and Why It Matters for Your Choice

In March 2026, the FDA sent warning letters to 30 telehealth companies selling compounded semaglutide and tirzepatide. The primary violations cited: claiming compounded versions were equivalent to branded FDA-approved drugs, and continuing to mass-compound after the shortage designation ended.

This wasn't a surprise to anyone watching the space. The FDA had been escalating enforcement since early 2025. What changed in 2026 is the speed and scope — and the fact that several major platforms that built their entire business on compounded GLP-1s were suddenly forced to pivot or shut down.

⚠️ If your current program received an FDA warning letter: This does not necessarily mean the program is shutting down — but it does mean they were found to be out of compliance. Ask your provider directly what changes they are making and whether your medication supply is at risk.

How to Choose the Right Program for You

With the regulatory landscape now clearer, here is a simple framework for choosing a GLP-1 telehealth program in 2026:

  1. Confirm the medication type — Are they offering FDA-approved branded medications or compounded alternatives? If compounded, ask specifically which pharmacy pathway (503A patient-specific vs 503B bulk) and whether there is documented clinical justification.
  2. Check for FDA warning letters — Search "[provider name] FDA warning letter 2026" before enrolling. This is public information.
  3. Verify state availability — Confirm your state is explicitly listed, not implied by "most states" language.
  4. Review the pricing before giving your card details — Any program that won't show you full pricing before enrollment is a yellow flag.
  5. Confirm physician involvement — A licensed physician should review your health history and make the prescribing decision — not an algorithm.
